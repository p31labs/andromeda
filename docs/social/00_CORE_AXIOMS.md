## Axiom 4: The SUBSAFE Origin

### The Loss of USS Thresher

On April 10, 1963, the nuclear-powered submarine USS Thresher (SSN-593) was lost at sea during a deep test dive off the northeastern coast of the United States, resulting in the tragic loss of all 129 military and civilian personnel aboard. The disaster was traced to the failure of a substandard silver-brazed salt-water piping joint exposed to full sea pressure. The catastrophic failure of this single joint caused uncontrollable flooding in the engine room, shorting out critical electrical panels, causing an automatic reactor scram, and resulting in the fatal loss of propulsion and ballast control.

A subsequent naval court of inquiry revealed profound, systemic failures that extended far beyond a single mechanical defect: deficient specifications, inadequate shipbuilding practices, and a critical, systemic absence of documentation and **Objective Quality Evidence (OQE)** regarding materials and processes utilized on the submarine. During the Thresher's final shipyard maintenance, ultrasonic testing had revealed that approximately 14 percent of inspected silver-brazed joints were substandard. Despite this alarming data, the vessel was permitted to deploy because risk management had fundamentally failed to keep pace with the platform's technological capabilities and operational depths.

### The SUBSAFE Program

In response, the Navy formally established the **Submarine Safety (SUBSAFE) program** on June 3, 1963 — 54 days after the loss. SUBSAFE provides "maximum reasonable assurance" of two non-negotiable imperatives:

1. The **watertight integrity** of the pressure hull.
2. The **operability of critical systems** required to control and recover from a flooding hazard.

The program intentionally restricts focus to these existential threats, excluding other safety domains to prevent dilution of its extreme rigor. Since December 1963, **not a single SUBSAFE-certified submarine has been lost** — a stark contrast to the pre-1963 average of one submarine lost every three years to noncombat causes.

The necessity of this rigor was tragically reinforced by the loss of USS Scorpion in 1968, which was **not** SUBSAFE-certified. Due to Cold War operational pressures, the Navy had allowed a reduced overhaul process, deferring required structural inspections and bypassing critical design changes.
