## 1.4 The SUBSAFE Origin

On April 10, 1963, USS Thresher (SSN-593) was lost at sea during a deep test dive, killing all 129 military and civilian personnel. Cause: a substandard silver-brazed piping joint failed under sea pressure, causing uncontrollable flooding, electrical shorts, reactor scram, and loss of propulsion.

The subsequent naval court of inquiry revealed systemic failures: deficient specifications, inadequate shipbuilding practices, and a critical absence of **Objective Quality Evidence (OQE)** — 14% of inspected joints were substandard, yet the vessel deployed with unverified integrity.

The Navy established the **Submarine Safety (SUBSAFE) program** on June 3, 1963. Since December 1963, **not a single SUBSAFE-certified submarine has been lost** — versus one submarine lost every three years from 1915-1963. The loss of USS Scorpion (1968, not SUBSAFE-certified) reinforced the doctrine: safety requirements must be followed regardless of operational pressure.

**Translation:** The SOULSAFE protocol (see [Part IV](#part-iv-the-soulsafe-protocol--cognitive-safety-doctrine)) transposes SUBSAFE principles from deep-ocean hydrostatic pressure to the high-velocity pressure of human-AI cognitive generation.
