The P31 Manifesto

Version 1.0.0 · Effective 2026-05-02 · Companion to CWP-P31-PEER-COMP-2026-05. Canonical source: docs/P31-MANIFESTO.md.

P31 Labs, Inc. is a Georgia nonprofit (EIN 42-1888158, 501(c)(3) pending) built around a single neurodivergent operator with hypoparathyroidism. We serve a four-person family mesh today, and we publish the substrate so any other family — or any person walking in cold — can use what we built.

This page says what we believe. Everything else in our repository is either an attempt to live by these principles, or a debt we owe the principles.

The five doctrines

These are not goals. They are the floor. Anything we ship that breaks one of them is broken on purpose, and we say so out loud.

  1. Operator-condition-aware AI. Every assistive persona we build carries the operator's medical and cognitive condition into its system prompt. No persona may invent a court docket, prescribe a medical action, treat executive dysfunction as a deficit of intelligence, or use the metaphors that hurt.
  2. The K₄ family mesh as architectural primitive. Four vertices. Six edges. Closed cage. The mesh is not a chat product. It scales by becoming many parallel small complete graphs.
  3. The Cognitive Passport as portable personalization without surveillance. The passport lives in the user's browser. Our servers never see it. The user generates it, owns it, deletes it.
  4. A measurable voice. The DELTA language and the Public Voice doctrine are written down, machine-checked, and CI-enforced. We forbid urgency manipulation and engagement-maximization patterns at the build step.
  5. Sub-medical-grade by design. P31 does not diagnose, prescribe, or substitute for clinical care. This is the architectural choice that lets a tool reach the people who need it most without a regulatory burden.

Ten principles

  1. The user owns their personalization. The Cognitive Passport ships in the browser. If you walk away, the data walks with you.
  2. We collect nothing we do not need. No analytics. No behavioral telemetry of any kind.
  3. Local-first, with a cloud floor. The Ollama persona fleet runs on the operator's own laptop. No cloud LLM is ever called from operator-confidential surfaces.
  4. Open by default. Source on GitHub. Contracts as JSON. Schemas versioned. License exceptions named in docs/LICENSE-POLICY.md.
  5. Plain language over performance. The avoid-list is in docs/PUBLIC-VOICE.md and the build fails if the words appear in watched copy.
  6. Accessibility is not a feature; it is the floor. WCAG 2.2 AA audit + remediation of all P0 / P1 findings. See /accessibility.
  7. Cryptography is borrowed, not invented. MLS (RFC 9420) for messaging. NIST FIPS 203 / 204 for post-quantum. WebAuthn for passkeys.
  8. Trust comes from third-party audit, not our own assurance. Phase 2 commits to commissioning an independent security audit and publishing the report.
  9. Money does not buy attention here. 0% platform fee. No advertising. No surveillance monetization. Free room access. 990 financial filings public.
  10. The mesh is small on purpose. We borrow practices from Mozilla, Signal, Apple, and Meta and reject the parts that do not match the shape of a four-person family mesh.

What we will not do

  • Collect phone numbers as identity, for verification, or later.
  • Measure daily active users, retention curves, or engagement metrics.
  • Ship a voice or video product on top of unencrypted transport.
  • Stand up a moderation team for chat-with-strangers — no such product exists in our scope.
  • Require an app to participate. Progressive Web App first; native only with grant funding.
  • Treat accessibility as an afterthought.
  • Add a cloud LLM endpoint to operator-confidential surfaces. Ever.

Closing

The four peer companies (Meta, Apple, Signal, Mozilla) have decades of practice between them. We have weeks. What we lack in scale, we close in transparency. What we lack in headcount, we close in plain documentation. What we cannot match in hardware, we make irrelevant by running on a fifty-dollar Chromebook.

The mesh holds because it is small, because it is honest, and because the people inside it can see the floor under their feet.

— W. Johnson, Operator · P31 Labs, Inc. · 2026-05-02
